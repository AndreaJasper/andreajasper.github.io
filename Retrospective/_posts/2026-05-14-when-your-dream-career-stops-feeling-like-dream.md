For most of my life, I thought I knew exactly what I wanted to be.

As a teenager, I became fascinated by websites and technology. What started as curiosity quickly turned into a long-term goal: someday, I was going to become a software developer.

That dream followed me for more than twenty years.

It influenced the jobs I pursued, the skills I learned, and eventually the education I invested in. Even when life took unexpected turns, the destination always felt the same.

Then something unexpected happened.

I got there.

And once I did, I realized the dream no longer fit the person I had become.

#  The Arrival
Like many long-term goals, becoming a developer became part of my identity.

In 2012, I landed a role as a project manager at a web design agency. While I wasn't writing code professionally, I worked closely with designers and developers and spent countless hours building my technical skills outside of work.

Over time, project management became my career, but software development remained the dream in the background.
Then in 2020, after being laid off during the pandemic, I decided to go all in.

I enrolled in a full-stack development bootcamp, completed the program, and eventually landed a software engineering role. After years of learning, practicing, and working toward that goal, I had finally achieved what I set out to do.

At least, that's what I thought.

#  The Unexpected Lesson
A few years later, I found myself feeling disconnected from the work.

At first, I assumed I was burned out. Maybe I needed a different project. Maybe I needed to learn another framework or programming language. Maybe I just needed to push a little harder.

But the more honest I became with myself, the harder it was to ignore a simple truth.

I enjoyed learning about technology more than I enjoyed building websites for a living.

That realization caught me completely off guard.

For years, I had treated becoming a developer as the finish line. I had spent so much time focused on reaching the destination that I never stopped to ask whether I actually enjoyed the day-to-day reality waiting for me once I arrived.

The answer wasn't entirely negative. I still enjoy coding. I still build projects for fun. I still love technology and the creativity that comes with solving problems.

What I didn't enjoy was the specific career path I had convinced myself was the only path worth pursuing.

# Permission to Evolve

Around the same time, I was reading The Subtle Art of Not Giving a Fck* by Mark Manson.

One idea from the book stuck with me: wanting a result isn't the same thing as enjoying the process required to achieve it.

That observation forced me to look at my own career differently.

For years, I had been chasing a version of success that made sense when I was sixteen. But the person who set that goal couldn't possibly know who I would become twenty years later.

Somewhere along the way, I had assumed that changing direction would mean I had failed.

Instead, I discovered the opposite.

Letting go of an old goal wasn't failure—it was growth.

The moment I stopped forcing myself into a version of success that no longer fit, I felt something I hadn't experienced in a long time:

Relief.

# Growing Through Every Version

That realization created space for new interests to emerge.

I rediscovered my appreciation for project management and collaboration. I became more curious about data, business, operations, and the ways organizations function behind the scenes. Topics I had once treated as secondary suddenly felt exciting again.

More importantly, I stopped viewing career decisions as permanent.

For a long time, I thought careers were supposed to follow a straight line. Pick a destination, commit to it, and stay the course no matter what.

Life rarely works that way.

We learn. We grow. We change.

The things that inspire us in one season of life may not inspire us in the next, and that's perfectly okay.

Looking back, I don't regret pursuing software development. The journey taught me valuable skills, introduced me to incredible people, and opened doors I never expected.

But one of the most important lessons it taught me had nothing to do with technology.

Growth isn't always about persistence.

Sometimes it's about permission.

Permission to change your mind.

Permission to outgrow old goals.

Permission to redefine success.

After all, we're all works in progress—growing through every version, one iteration at a time.

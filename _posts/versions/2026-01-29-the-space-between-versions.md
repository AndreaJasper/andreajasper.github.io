Lately, I've been thinking about how much of my identity became wrapped up in my career.

For years, introducing myself was easy. I was a project manager. It wasn't just my job title—it was how I understood myself. I was the person with the plan, the timeline, the budget, and the backup plan for when things inevitably went sideways.

Then life interrupted.

My layoff at the beginning of COVID didn't just take away a job. It removed a version of myself I'd spent more than a decade building. Suddenly, the career I'd carefully cultivated was gone, and for the first time in a long time, I felt completely untethered.

In an attempt to regain some control, I pursued a dream I'd carried with me since I was a teenager: becoming a software engineer.

I threw myself into learning, landed a role, and finally achieved something I'd wanted for years.

But somewhere along the way, I learned an uncomfortable truth.

The dream I had at sixteen wasn't necessarily the dream I wanted at thirty-nine.

I enjoyed coding. I still do. I love building things, solving problems, and exploring new ideas. But I discovered that I enjoyed it most as a hobby—a place to create without deadlines, clients, or expectations.

What I missed was the bigger picture.

I missed bringing people together to solve problems. I missed the messy beginnings of a project, the coordination, the strategy, and even the occasional fire drill that appears despite everyone's best efforts.

Then the tech industry shifted again.

Like many others, I found myself searching for direction while competing in a crowded job market. I spent months sending applications, revising resumes, and trying to figure out where I fit.

And that's when a question kept surfacing:

If I'm not a project manager, and I'm not a software engineer, then who am I?

For a long time, I thought I needed an answer.

Now I'm not so sure.

Over the last year, I've started to realize that maybe the problem wasn't changing careers. Maybe the problem was believing my career and my identity were the same thing.

A job can be part of who we are, but it shouldn't be the whole story.

At the end of 2025, I decided to try something new. After spending fifteen years in tech, I enrolled in college and began exploring a long-standing interest in business.

It's exciting.

It's intimidating.

And if I'm being honest, it's also a little strange to be a beginner again.

For the first time in a long time, I don't know exactly where the path leads.

I don't have a detailed roadmap or a five-year plan.

What I do have is curiosity.

And right now, that's enough.

I'm learning to become comfortable in the space between versions of myself—the place where one chapter has ended and the next hasn't fully taken shape.

It's uncertain, sometimes uncomfortable, and occasionally messy.

But it's also where growth happens.

And for the first time in a while, I'm looking forward to finding out who I become next.
